1. Welcome & Overview

Welcome to one of Ontario's most unique and specialized clinical experiences in reproductive medicine. This selective offers you a rare opportunity to observe and learn about the intersection of reproductive endocrinology, fertility preservation, and oncofertility care.

Understanding the depth of subspecialization in this field

By the end of this selective, you will be able to:

  • Recognize the unique considerations in oncofertility care, including fertility preservation options (particularly ovarian tissue cryopreservation), multidisciplinary approaches for pediatric and adult cancer patients, and post-treatment reproductive counseling for cancer survivors
  • Gain insight into the clinician-investigator role, balancing clinical excellence with scholarly work including research, manuscript preparation, and academic presentations

These are hands-on clinical days where you'll observe the full spectrum of reproductive procedures and patient care. Procedures You Will Observe:

Operative Hysteroscopy

What it is: A minimally invasive surgical procedure to diagnose and treat intrauterine pathology using a hysteroscope (a thin, lighted telescope) inserted through the cervix.

Common indications: Removal of uterine polyps, submucosal fibroids, uterine septum, or intrauterine adhesions that may impair fertility.

Clinical significance: Optimizing the uterine cavity is crucial before embryo transfer or attempting conception.

Oocyte Retrieval (Egg Retrieval)

What it is: An ultrasound-guided transvaginal procedure to aspirate mature oocytes from ovarian follicles after controlled ovarian stimulation.

Context: This is performed for both infertility treatment (IVF) and fertility preservation (oocyte cryopreservation).

What you'll observe: Patient preparation, conscious sedation, ultrasound guidance, aspiration technique, and immediate embryology lab communication.

Embryo Transfer

What it is: The final step of IVF where a selected embryo is transferred into the uterus using a thin catheter under ultrasound guidance.

What you'll observe: The delicate technique required, ultrasound visualization, catheter selection, and the "gentle touch" approach that maximizes success.

Sonohysterogram (Saline Infusion Sonography)

What it is: An ultrasound procedure where sterile saline is infused into the uterine cavity to better visualize the endometrium and detect pathology.

Indications: Evaluation of abnormal uterine bleeding, suspected polyps, fibroids, uterine anomalies, and tubal patency testing.

Office Hysteroscopy

What it is: Diagnostic visualization of the uterine cavity using a narrow hysteroscope in the office setting without anesthesia.

When performed: Initial assessment of uterine cavity, evaluation of abnormal bleeding, confirmation of suspected intrauterine pathology.

3. Core Fertility Concepts

Before diving into the specialized world of fertility preservation and oncofertility, it's helpful to understand some foundational concepts in reproductive endocrinology and infertility.

Basic Reproductive Physiology

The Menstrual Cycle

Understanding the menstrual cycle is fundamental to all fertility care:

  • Follicular Phase: FSH stimulates follicle growth; naturally only one dominant follicle emerges
  • Ovulation: LH surge triggers the release of a mature oocyte (around day 14)
  • Luteal Phase: Corpus luteum produces progesterone to support a potential pregnancy

Ovarian Reserve

Definition: The quantity of oocytes remaining in the ovaries. This is NOT a predictor of fertility potential! But it is an important marker for how many oocytes can be obtained in fertility treatment, helpful in determining the dose of medications and treatment protocols.

Assessment methods:

  • Antral Follicle Count (AFC): Ultrasound count of small follicles (2-10mm) on days 2-5 of cycle, in both ovaries
  • Anti-Müllerian Hormone (AMH): Blood test that correlates with ovarian reserve; can be done any time in cycle. Dr. Michaeli's calculator: https://amh.fertilitypreservation.ca/
  • FSH & Estradiol: Day 2-3 levels; elevated FSH suggests diminished reserve, not as commonly used as a marker

Why Ovarian Reserve Matters in Oncofertility

Cancer treatments (chemotherapy, radiation) can significantly damage ovarian reserve. The extent of damage depends on the patient's age, baseline reserve, and the degree of gonadotoxicity of the treatment regimen. This is why fertility preservation before treatment is so critical, and follow-up in survivorship can help guide family planning and possible preservation options in the future.

Controlled Ovarian Stimulation

This is the process of using medications to stimulate multiple follicles to grow simultaneously — essential for both IVF and fertility preservation.

Key Processes and Medications Involved:

Ovarian stimulation involves three main phases using medications for stimulation and suppression of premature ovulation:

  1. Stimulation Phase: Using gonadotropins to promote multiple follicle development
  2. Suppression Phase: Preventing premature ovulation with GnRH antagonists or agonists
  3. Trigger Phase: Inducing final oocyte maturation before retrieval

Key Medications:

  • Gonadotropins (FSH/LH): Injectable hormones that stimulate follicle development
  • GnRH Antagonists: Prevent premature ovulation (e.g., Cetrotide, Ganirelix)
  • GnRH Agonists: Can be used for "trigger" (final oocyte maturation) or for pituitary suppression
  • hCG Trigger: Mimics the natural LH surge to trigger final egg maturation 36 hours before retrieval

Random Start Protocols

Important for oncofertility: Traditional IVF cycles start on day 2-3 of menses. However, cancer patients can't always wait. Random start (or luteal phase start) protocols allow stimulation to begin at any point in the cycle — critical when treatment must begin urgently.

Key Terminology

  • AFC (Antral Follicle Count): Number of small follicles visible on ultrasound; marker of ovarian reserve
  • Agonist: Medication that activates a receptor (e.g., GnRH agonist)
  • AMH (Anti-Müllerian Hormone): Blood test measuring ovarian reserve
  • Antagonist: Medication that blocks a receptor (e.g., GnRH antagonist)
  • Atresia: Natural degeneration of follicles
  • Corpus Luteum: Structure formed after ovulation that produces progesterone
  • Cryopreservation: Freezing for storage
  • Follicle: Fluid-filled sac in the ovary containing an oocyte
  • FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone): Hormone that stimulates follicle growth
  • GnRH (Gonadotropin-Releasing Hormone): Brain hormone that controls FSH/LH release
  • Gonadotoxic: Toxic to the gonads (ovaries/testes); refers to treatments that can damage fertility
  • hCG (Human Chorionic Gonadotropin): Hormone used to trigger final egg maturation
  • LH (Luteinizing Hormone): Hormone that triggers ovulation
  • Oocyte: Egg cell
  • OTC (Ovarian Tissue Cryopreservation): Freezing ovarian cortex for future transplantation
  • Primordial Follicles: Microscopic "dormant" follicles representing the true ovarian reserve; cannot be directly measured, but correlates with functional ovarian reserve
  • Vitrification: Ultra-rapid freezing technique used for egg and embryo cryopreservation
  • Advanced Reproductive Age: Typically refers to women aged 35 and older, when fertility naturally declines and pregnancy risks increase
  • COS-IUI (Controlled Ovarian Stimulation with Intrauterine Insemination): Fertility treatment combining ovarian stimulation medications with timed placement of sperm directly into the uterus
  • Diminished Ovarian Reserve (DOR): Reduced quantity and/or quality of oocytes, indicated by low AMH, low AFC, or elevated FSH levels
  • IVF (In Vitro Fertilization): Fertility treatment where eggs are retrieved, fertilized with sperm in a laboratory, and resulting embryos are transferred to the uterus
  • Male Factor Infertility: Infertility caused by issues with sperm production, function, or delivery; accounts for approximately 40-50% of infertility cases
  • Ovulation Induction: Use of medications (letrozole, clomiphene/Clomid, or gonadotropins) to stimulate ovulation in women who don't ovulate regularly
  • POI (Premature Ovarian Insufficiency): Loss of ovarian function before age 40, also known as premature menopause; can result from cancer treatment, autoimmune conditions, or genetic factors
  • Third Party Reproduction: Use of donated eggs, sperm, embryos, or gestational carriers (surrogates) to achieve pregnancy when using one's own gametes is not possible
  • Unexplained Infertility: Infertility diagnosis when standard testing fails to identify a specific cause; accounts for approximately 15-30% of infertility cases

4. Fertility Preservation & Oncofertility

Oncofertility is an emerging field that bridges oncology and reproductive medicine, focusing on preserving and restoring fertility in cancer patients and survivors.

Why Fertility Preservation Matters

Approximately 3,000 patients below age 39 are diagnosed with cancer in Ontario annually. Many are in their reproductive years when fertility is a significant life priority. Cancer treatments can cause:

  • In females: Permanent ovarian failure (premature menopause), diminished ovarian reserve, DNA damage to remaining oocytes, uterine damage from radiation
  • In males: Reduced sperm production, azoospermia (complete absence of sperm), DNA damage to sperm
  • Psychosocial impact: Severe fertility-related distress and concerns about future family building, which can significantly affect quality of life and cancer survivorship

The window for fertility preservation is narrow — ideally, it must occur before gonadotoxic treatment begins. However, assessment of fertility function in survivorship can offer additional opportunities for fertility preservation and help plan family building strategies to avoid missed opportunities and regret.

Patient Populations

Cancer Patients

  • Newly diagnosed: Fertility preservation before chemotherapy/radiation
  • Survivors: Assessment and reproductive counseling after treatment
  • Pediatric patients: Special considerations for pre-pubertal children and adolescents

Other Indications

  • Benign conditions requiring gonadotoxic treatment: Hemoglobinopathies (Thalassemia, Sickle Cell Disease), severe autoimmune disease, bone marrow transplant for non-malignant conditions
  • Gender-affirming care: Fertility preservation before gender-affirming hormone therapy or surgery
  • Planned egg freezing: Elective oocyte cryopreservation

Fertility Preservation Options

Established Options (Standard of Care)

Oocyte Cryopreservation (Egg Freezing)

Best for: Post-pubertal females with adequate time for ovarian stimulation (typically 2 weeks)

Process: Controlled ovarian stimulation → oocyte retrieval → vitrification

Advantages: No partner/sperm needed; eggs belong to patient alone

Success rates: Age-dependent; generally 30-60% live birth rate per ~15-20 mature eggs

Considerations: Requires 10-14 days; hormone-sensitive cancers may need special protocols

Embryo Cryopreservation

Best for: Patients with a partner or using donor sperm

Process: Same as oocyte cryopreservation, but eggs are fertilized before freezing

Advantages: Embryo is closer to the target of live birth, providing a more realistic estimation of success with less uncertainty about outcomes. Success rates of embryos and eggs are now considered equivalent.

Considerations: Requires partner/donor decision; legal/ethical considerations

Ovarian Tissue Cryopreservation (OTC)

Best for:

  • Pre-pubertal girls who cannot undergo ovarian stimulation
  • Patients who cannot delay cancer treatment for 2 weeks
  • Very aggressive chemotherapy regimens likely to cause complete ovarian failure

The Procedure:

  • Laparoscopic removal of part or whole ovary
  • Ovarian cortex (outer layer containing primordial follicles) is dissected into strips
  • Tissue is cryopreserved using slow-freeze protocols
  • Can be done any time in menstrual cycle
  • Usually takes 1-2 hours; patient goes home same day
  • Remote tissue harvesting: Tissue can be harvested at remote sites and transported to Mount Sinai Fertility for processing — this is the only program in Canada offering this service, bringing access to care across the province

Future use:

  • Orthotopic transplantation: Tissue grafted back into pelvic cavity near remaining ovary or at ovarian site
  • Heterotopic transplantation: Grafted to accessible site like forearm (research)

Current status: Over 200 live births worldwide from OTC. No longer considered experimental by ASRM (as of 2019). Success rates are similar to embryo and egg freezing, but these are international reports, and there is no Canadian baby from ovarian tissue re-transplantation since it has not been attempted yet.

Sperm Cryopreservation (Sperm Freezing)

Best for: Post-pubertal males before gonadotoxic treatment

Process: Semen sample collected via masturbation, processed, and cryopreserved

Advantages: Simple, non-invasive procedure; can be done quickly before treatment

Considerations: May be challenging for adolescents; multiple samples recommended when time permits

Testicular Sperm Extraction

Best for: Males unable to produce ejaculated sperm (azoospermia, neurological conditions)

Methods:

  • Micro-TESE (Microdissection Testicular Sperm Extraction): Surgical procedure using microscope to identify seminiferous tubules most likely to contain sperm
  • Electroejaculation: Electrical stimulation to induce ejaculation in patients with spinal cord injuries or neurological conditions preventing natural ejaculation

Emerging/Experimental Options

In Vitro Maturation (IVM)

What it is: Collection of immature oocytes from unstimulated or minimally stimulated ovaries, followed by maturation in the laboratory

Potential advantages: Avoids ovarian stimulation, can be performed urgently

Status: Lower success rates than conventional IVF; currently only offered experimentally in the context of OTC for ovarian tissue-derived oocytes

Uterine Transposition and Transplantation

Uterine transposition: Surgical repositioning of the uterus outside the radiation field to minimize damage during pelvic radiation

Uterine transplantation: Transplanting a donor uterus to enable pregnancy in women born without a uterus or who lost uterine function

Status: Currently not performed in Canada; several successful pregnancies reported worldwide from uterine transplantation

Gonadotoxicity Risk Stratification

Different cancer treatments carry different risks to fertility:

Risk Level Examples Estimated Risk
High Risk Cyclophosphamide (high dose), whole abdominal/pelvic radiation, total body irradiation, bone marrow transplant conditioning >80% risk of premature ovarian insufficiency
Intermediate Risk Doxorubicin-based regimens, cyclophosphamide (moderate dose), carboplatin Variable, age-dependent
Low/Unknown Risk Vincristine, methotrexate, 5-FU, targeted therapies <20% risk

Age is the most important modifier: A 38-year-old receiving "low-risk" chemotherapy may still experience ovarian failure, while a 22-year-old receiving "high-risk" treatment may retain some ovarian function.

The Cancer Survivorship Fertility Clinic

This specialized clinic serves patients who have completed cancer treatment and are now considering their reproductive options.

What you'll observe:

  • Ovarian reserve assessment: AFC, AMH testing to determine remaining ovarian reserve
  • Counseling on conception timing: How long to wait after treatment; considerations for recurrence risk, reproductive potential assessment
  • Discussion of pregnancy safety: Cancer-specific considerations (e.g., breast cancer and pregnancy), gonadotoxic exposure and children's health, genetic predisposition to cancer (BRCA and other genetic conditions)
  • Use of preserved tissue/eggs/embryos: Discussing next steps for those who preserved fertility
  • Egg/sperm donation counseling: For those with ovarian/testicular insufficiency
  • Third-party reproduction options: Gestational carrier for those who cannot safely carry a pregnancy

Pediatric Fertility Preservation

Fertility preservation in children presents unique medical, ethical, and psychological challenges. The Fertility Preservation Program at SickKids provides this unique care in partnership with Mount Sinai Fertility.

Special Considerations:

  • Consent/assent: Balancing parental consent with child assent when appropriate
  • Pre-pubertal patients: Limited options (OTC is primary option)
  • Post-pubertal patients: Similar options to adults, but additional psychological support needed
  • Timing pressures: Must balance oncologic urgency with fertility preservation
  • Uncertainty: Unknown future fertility desires; talking about sexuality/reproduction with children/adolescents
  • Multidisciplinary approach: Pediatric fertility preservation requires coordination between pediatric oncologists, reproductive endocrinologists, pediatric gynecologists, social workers and psychologists, bioethicists, and the child's family

Ethical Considerations

Oncofertility raises profound ethical questions that I invite you to explore:

  • Balancing life-saving cancer treatment urgency with fertility preservation
  • Informed consent when patients are in crisis
  • Disposition of stored tissue/eggs/embryos if the patient doesn't survive cancer
  • Cost and access to care (fertility preservation often incurs unexpected additional expenses that are not covered by insurance)
  • Posthumous reproduction from stored gametes
  • Use of experimental procedures in vulnerable populations
